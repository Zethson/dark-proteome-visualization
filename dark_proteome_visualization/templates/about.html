About the Dark Proteome

The dark proteome describes the set of proteins that cannot be observed by experimental structure determination or is inaccessable to homology modeling. A recent study finds that about half of the proteins in eukaryotes and viruses are dark, while about an eigth of all archaea and bacteria proteins are. Since dark proteins are suspected to be related to hard-to-cure diseases such as Parkinson, cancer or Alzheimer, gaining insights into the structure of dark proteins can improve the research speed and quality.

Due to the massive amount of data that quickly accumulates when working in Bioinformatics, it is important to make the data accessable. One way to do that is by smart visualizations, from which domain experts can extract important knowledge to develop new research.

For this website, we use the data from the SwissProt, which provides information about sequenced proteins in two tables: the dark_proteins_all.csv, which contains 546000 samples and information about the primary accession, domain, kingdom, organism id, darkness, length, disorder, compositional bias and membrane as well as the dark_proteome.csv, which contains 20209 samples and information about primary accession, uncertainty, length, disorder, compositional bias and membrane.

For more information about the dark proteome in general, we refer to the human dark proteome initiative, that promotes research on dark proteins.
